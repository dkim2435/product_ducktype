The History of Keyboards and Typing

Published February 12, 2026 · 5 min read

Every time you sit down at a computer and start typing, you are using a tool with over 150 years of history behind it. The modern keyboard might feel like a natural extension of computing, but its design, layout, and mechanics have been shaped by decades of innovation, debate, and cultural change. Understanding how keyboards evolved helps us appreciate why we type the way we do today and where typing culture might be heading next.

The Birth of the Typewriter

The story of the keyboard begins in 1868, when Christopher Latham Sholes, along with Carlos Glidden and Samuel Soule, patented an early writing machine in Milwaukee, Wisconsin. Sholes is widely credited as the inventor of the first practical typewriter, though many earlier attempts had been made by inventors around the world. His design used a set of typebars arranged in a circular basket, each striking an inked ribbon to leave an impression on paper.

Early prototypes had their keys arranged alphabetically, but this caused a significant mechanical problem. When adjacent typebars were pressed in quick succession, they would jam together. Sholes spent years rearranging the layout to reduce these jams, eventually arriving at the QWERTY arrangement we still use today. In 1873, Sholes sold his patent to Remington and Sons, a firearms manufacturer looking to diversify. The Remington No. 1 typewriter hit the market in 1874, and the age of mechanical typing had begun.

The typewriter transformed communication, business, and even social dynamics. It created an entirely new profession, the typist, and was one of the first technologies that opened office work to women in large numbers. Typing schools sprang up across America and Europe, teaching the touch-typing method that is still the gold standard for efficient keyboard use.

The QWERTY Layout Takes Hold

By the early 1900s, QWERTY had become the dominant keyboard layout worldwide. Not because it was the fastest or most ergonomic arrangement, but because of something economists call path dependence. Once millions of typists had learned QWERTY, and once thousands of typing schools were teaching it, switching to a different layout carried enormous costs. Typewriter manufacturers standardized on QWERTY, and each new generation of typists reinforced its dominance.

There have been many attempts to unseat QWERTY over the years. August Dvorak introduced his Simplified Keyboard in 1936, claiming it was faster and more comfortable. Colemak appeared in 2006 as a modern compromise. But QWERTY has proven remarkably persistent. Today it remains the default on virtually every English-language keyboard, computer, and smartphone in the world.

The IBM Model M and the Golden Age of Mechanical Keyboards

When computing moved from typewriters to electronic terminals in the 1960s and 1970s, keyboards evolved with it. Early computer keyboards used various switch technologies, but the most iconic keyboard of the era was the IBM Model M, introduced in 1985. Built with buckling spring switches, the Model M produced a satisfying tactile click and a distinctive sound that many typists came to love.

The Model M was built to last. Its heavy steel backplate and thick keycaps made it feel solid and reliable. Many original Model M keyboards are still in use today, over 40 years later. It set the standard for what a quality typing experience should feel like and became a benchmark that keyboard enthusiasts still reference.

During this era, typing became an essential computer skill. As personal computers entered homes and offices in the 1980s and 1990s, millions of people learned to type not on typewriters but on computer keyboards. Typing software like Mavis Beacon Teaches Typing became bestsellers, and keyboarding classes became a staple of school curricula.

The Rise of Rubber Dome and Membrane Keyboards

As the PC market expanded in the 1990s, manufacturers faced pressure to cut costs. Mechanical switch keyboards were expensive to produce, so most companies switched to rubber dome and membrane technology. These keyboards used a sheet of rubber with dome-shaped bumps under each key. Pressing a key collapsed the dome, pushing two membrane layers together to register the keystroke.

Rubber dome keyboards were cheap, quiet, and lightweight. They became the default keyboard bundled with desktop computers and were used in billions of laptops. But they sacrificed the tactile feedback and durability that mechanical switches provided. For many typists, the feel of typing became mushy and imprecise. Typing speed and comfort suffered, though most users never knew what they were missing because they had never used anything else.

The Mechanical Keyboard Renaissance

Starting around 2010, a grassroots movement of keyboard enthusiasts began rediscovering mechanical switches. Online communities on forums and Reddit shared their passion for keyboards with Cherry MX switches, custom keycaps, and DIY builds. What started as a niche hobby quickly grew into a global phenomenon.

Today the mechanical keyboard market offers an overwhelming variety of options:

  • Cherry MX switches in linear (Red), tactile (Brown), and clicky (Blue) variants
  • Custom switches from manufacturers like Gateron, Kailh, and Durock
  • Hot-swappable boards that let you change switches without soldering
  • Custom keycap sets in every color, profile, and material imaginable
  • Group buys and limited runs that make keyboards collectible items

The mechanical keyboard renaissance has had a measurable impact on typing culture. More people than ever are paying attention to how their keyboard feels, sounds, and performs. Typing tests and speed competitions have grown in popularity alongside the keyboard hobby, with many enthusiasts chasing higher WPM scores on their custom setups.

Ergonomic Keyboards and Health

As computers became central to work life, repetitive strain injuries (RSI) and carpal tunnel syndrome became widespread concerns. This led to the development of ergonomic keyboards designed to reduce strain on the hands, wrists, and arms. Microsoft introduced its Natural Keyboard in 1994, featuring a split and tented design that angled the two halves of the keyboard to match the natural position of the hands.

Since then, ergonomic keyboard design has branched into many directions. Split keyboards like the Kinesis Advantage and ErgoDox place each hand's keys on a separate unit, allowing full customization of position and angle. Columnar layouts align keys in straight columns rather than staggered rows, reducing finger travel. Tenting mechanisms angle the keyboard halves inward to keep wrists in a neutral position.

For anyone who types for hours each day, ergonomic considerations are not just about comfort but about long-term health. Investing in a well-designed keyboard and learning proper typing posture can prevent injuries that might otherwise end a career.

Modern Innovations and the Future

Keyboard innovation continues at a rapid pace. Low-profile mechanical switches bring the feel of mechanical keyboards to slim laptop-style designs. Wireless technology has freed keyboards from cable clutter, with Bluetooth and 2.4 GHz connections offering near-zero latency. Programmable firmware like QMK and VIA lets users customize every aspect of their keyboard's behavior, from key mappings to lighting effects.

Some of the most exciting developments are happening at the intersection of keyboards and software. Adaptive typing algorithms can adjust key sensitivity based on your typing patterns. AI-powered autocorrect and predictive text are changing how we interact with virtual keyboards on touchscreens. And typing analytics tools like DuckType help people understand and improve their typing performance with detailed metrics and targeted practice.

The keyboard has come a long way from Sholes' mechanical contraption in 1868, but its core purpose remains the same: to translate human thought into written words as quickly and accurately as possible.

Whether you are typing on a vintage Model M, a custom-built mechanical board, or a laptop membrane keyboard, you are part of a tradition that stretches back over a century and a half. The tools may change, but the skill of typing remains one of the most valuable abilities in the digital age.
